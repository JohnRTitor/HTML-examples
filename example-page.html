GYMNOSPERMS

Gymnosperms are vascular plants that reproduce via exposed seeds or ovules, unlike angiosperms, whose seeds are enclosed by fruits. Their seeds, often found in cones, are visible only at maturity. There are four divisions of gymnosperms: Pinophyta, Cycadophyta, Ginkgophyta, and Gnetophyta, comprising 88 genera and over 1,000 species worldwide. Gymnosperms were dominant during the Mesozoic Era and, although they have been largely replaced by angiosperms since the Cretaceous Period, they still thrive in many regions. Conifer forests, for example, span large areas of northern temperate lands.

General Features of Gymnosperms

The visible part of gymnosperms is the sporophyte, representing the asexual generation. A typical sporophyte has a stem, roots, leaves, and reproductive structures. As vascular plants, gymnosperms have xylem and phloem tissues. The xylem conducts water and minerals from roots, providing structural support, while the phloem distributes nutrients from the leaves to the rest of the plant.
